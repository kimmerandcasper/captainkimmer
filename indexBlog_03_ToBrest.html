To Brest - Full System Failure!

02/14/2025

What a crazy trip this turned out to be. Before leaving, I had to do a few things—most importantly, get a latte from this gentleman who makes the best coffee I've had in Europe so far!

This is from the restaurant right above the Port Office in Cherbourg. Gonna miss it.

If you haven't been to Europe, know that these beautiful little pleasures called lattes are nearly impossible to find!

It was a little too windy to put up the new headsail, but with the help of a few locals, I got it done. They didn't speak English, but they were eager to help, partially because they had definitely seen the rescue propaganda and had a few laughs when they recognized me!

I don't know why, but this awesome new furling line (the red line) that furls and unfurls my headsail makes me happy!

I had to manually wind it around the recoil because we put the headsail on incorrectly. And my awesome friend Goran is holding the camera!

And after we met with Jan, who let me film a quick tour of his amazing ship—the *Jan Wilde Zwaan*! After that, we had drinks, and he fed Goran and me all sorts of cheese! It was difficult to leave; we were having so much fun, and his ship is extremely huge and comfy. But Goran and I wanted to go to a restaurant in town.

Warning! A little graphic.

After all the chemo, my organs don't process alcohol the same. I only had three drinks with Goran and Jan, spread out over 4+ hours. But I spent all night throwing up the delicious meal Goran and I had last night. It wasn't pleasant, and being hungover is not the way you want to sail. But I had to leave!

I'm not sentimental at all, but I truly was sad to leave Cherbourg. I really wish I could have spent much more time there, and despite them charging me too much for the visit and the fake rescue, it's one of my favorite places on Earth.

First thing, need to strap down the autopilot. Hopefully, it stays in place this time...

Leaving Cherbourg and its great harbor walls!

This great wall, which Napoleon had the French build, is beautiful. It was constructed in the early 19th century as part of his grand plan to turn Cherbourg into an impregnable naval base. Spanning nearly 4 kilometers, the breakwater took decades to complete and required thousands of workers. Napoleon himself visited the construction site multiple times, determined to create a safe harbor for his fleet. Today, it still stands as one of the most impressive maritime defenses in Europe!

Also, Goran informed me before I left that this was the last port the Titanic was at before attempting to cross the Atlantic Ocean. After research I believe it was actually the second to last stop, but still... kinda erie.

My VHF collision alarm was going off nonstop! Luckily, I figured out how to turn it off. Thank you, ChatGPT!

And, of course, I'm very excited for this trip, which I believe is going to go super smoothly!

And the mainsail won't go up—what the heck!

Oh no! The main halyard is wrapped around the mast light.

Me, eating the last of the Domino's I have, thinking about what to do with the main halyard. Nothing I've tried so far works! And though Jan lent me his harness so Goran could hoist me, I don't know how to climb the mast on my own.

Let's release the halyard from the mainsail, pull it up around the mast light, and let it back down.

Well, the wind made the halyard irrecoverable. But I'm not turning back! Good thing I had the headsail repaired because it's going to carry me to Brest!

You can just see the halyard at the top of the mast. I'll have to get it later.

Fishing buoys?

I have to make sure I tack away from these rocks.

Well, that did not go well. I tacked, but slowly—too slow to have enough speed to make the full turn to catch the other side of the wind. So then we lost our wind power, and the current was drifting us toward the rocks. As I'm fixing my sunglasses, I explain the madness of having the engine fail to start as I narrowly miss obstructions in the water. Luckily, ChatGPT came in clutch, explaining that there's this red button on the throttle that needs a push to de-neutralize the diesel engine's start before turning it over.

Not pretty! Those circles are the rocks.

Back to doing some sailing!

Yawn! America!

Dang, we're cruising!

Is it the wind? No. Looks pretty normal. I must just be an awesome one sail, sailor!

Not really. Turns out I hit some incredible currents near the more open Atlantic, and the waves were nearly as big as the storm I was in! Yet, the weather was calm. Look at the crest of this wave—it's much bigger than my boat!

And then I turned around an island, and the ocean was suddenly smooth like glass.

As you can see from the flag, we've lost the wind.

Might as well put the sail away.

So I get to try pulling my new furling line!

After a few hours of drifting, I start contemplating what I'm getting out of all this... Why am I out here?

Luckily, some good, yummy peas, pasta, and tuna cheered me up!

Coughing in the night. Still pretty sick. And I ran the autopilot with the sail out—turns out that was a big mistake!

Turns out, with the headsail, the wind, and the angle I had the autopilot steering, we were actually hove to.

Which means we didn't move from this spot all night.

Here are my vitamins. Trying to stay healthy. My dad encourages me to be on the vitamin train, and I think it does help, so thanks, Dad!

I'm going to make coffee!

Then get to work!

Still no wind... I think I'm going to run the engine. I don't like doing this—I want to sail. But it's time to move.

The engine won't start... Remember the autopilot last night? I fried the batteries. Both of them.

I have to let the solar panels charge them up. Good thing it's sunny. And now I have a lot of time for chores.

Like cleaning!

Hmmm, it's not charging as usual. I think I might have damaged the batteries :/ Justine told me this could happen if I over-drained them.

My wolfies are drooping!

That's better!

And I tripped outside while bringing my noodles out to strain them. I lost most of my noodles in the cockpit.

It's not charging fast enough. I need to tilt the panel so it faces the sun!

Seems like a good time to start reading the Bible.

At least I'm drifting in the right direction.

After a few hours, the engine still won't start.

So, more Bible.

Which worked! It's difficult to tell, but the shaft is blurred because it's spinning! That means the engine is running!

Thank you, God!

Still no wind. Good thing we're motoring.

I'm using this engine power to charge every device I have just in case.

Even my VHF radio is charging! Crazy. I don't know why it wasn't working before.

And I spilled my noodles again. This time I went to strain them and threw most of them into the ocean.

Goran helped me put my flag up on the backstay. Now I'm going to try using this mooring rod to push it higher up the cable.

And it looks pretty good next to the French courtesy flag Goran bought me. Apparently, you're supposed to fly a courtesy flag of the country you're visiting. I didn't know!

He also got me these awesome oranges!

Blue everywhere!

What?! I'm out of gas!? Turns out the starter battery needs to be on to get a reading. Phew!

Still not getting a great charge, even with the engine running. The batteries definitely have a little damage.

Here's the plan: motor closer to shore, get cell service, and do some research. Then, game plan again.

Almost there.

Yep. My bad. Sorry, batteries. I was too hard on you.

Look at that cool ship!

The sun's going down.

All my systems are off.

And we're moving.

At least it's beautiful outside!

It was a long night in my sleeping bag out in the cockpit, manually steering the whole time. I taped up the mooring rod to the tiller to give it more length. Much more comfortable!

When we were close enough, I set adrift and slept. Of course, I had to legally keep my ship's lights on.

It's daylight! Looks like the sun is out. We got lucky.

But not for long. The engine needs to start soon, or that storm will block out the sun.

We got it running!

Thank you, Jesus!

You can see some of the lighthouses that guided me through the night along the French coast.

And we're moving with the currents!

Dolphins! They played with my ship for 30 minutes.

Lots of rocks on this path.

But beautiful.

I leaned on the boom and bent it a little. Dang. I fixed it later, but for a second, I thought I did serious damage.

Other boaters were zooming across the ocean everywhere.

Look at those windmills!

And this thing! I think it monitors the weather and sea state.

Beautiful French town!

And a church with a lighthouse?

Dramatic pensiveness.

Weird scenario here. We were running with the current, so moving fast! But the wind was going in the opposite direction, causing the waves to move against the water flow, which resulted in me getting sprayed a lot!

My lighthouses! With WWII lookout bunkers.

This ship was cruising! It screamed past me.

The first sight of Brest.

And this giant research ship.

Time to get ready to dock.

And look professional, radioing in on the now charged VHF.

Getting ready to dock in new ports always makes me nervous.

But I got it done! Made it safely to Brest in three days! Glad to be here—Brest turns out to be one of my all-time favorite places.
